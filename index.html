Software Commons Consortium

The Software Commons Consortium (SCC) promotes sharing in the software industry.

Definition of the Software Commons

From Wikipedia:

The software commons consists of all computer software which is available at little or no cost and which can be reused with few restrictions. It includes open source software which can be modified with few restrictions. However the commons also includes software outside of these categories â€“ for instance, software which is in the public domain.

Mini-manifesto

We believe that public software sharing is a good practice, for [reasons]. The established brand related to software sharing, Open Source, is interpreted by many to preclude certain kinds of sharing that we nevertheless wish to promote. SCC recognizes the definitions of the Free and Open Source Software (FOSS) movement, while widening our focus to encompass additional sharing practices within the Software Commons.

We especially encourage companies to share their core software products under licenses that grant extensive freedoms while protecting their commercial interests.

Source Available is a related but highly ambiguous category, encompassing much software that is not part of the Software Commons (for example, code that is readable by customers under NDA but not by the public).

Our long-term goal is to grow the Software Commons into a brand as strong as Open Source. Our ideal future headline is along the lines of, “Microsoft Windows is now part of the Software Commons.”

Types of Software Sharing

SCC recognizes several broad categories of sharing practices in the software industry. All software released under any of these practices is part of the Software Commons.

Public Domain and equivalents
Fewest restrictions. (CC0, WTFPL)
Permissive
Few restrictions. (BSD, MIT, ISC, Apache)
Single-origin
Restrictions on competition. (FSL, BUSL, ELv2)
Open Core
Restrictions on feature set. (GitLab model)
Copyleft
Restrictions to enforce reciprocal sharing. (GPL, AGPL, LGPL, MPL)

Cramer's Notes

The intent here is to normalize a category of software which provides more freedom than closed source, and more freedom than typical source-available software. It should not diminish the value of FOSS (or OSI's definition of "Open Source"), but should also not be overly confusing to end-users, and not include prejudice.

We need a way to talk about licenses like FSL, BUSL, and arguably even things like Elastic. They cannot be called Open Source (and we should make that clear in our faq/whatever). They are not Closed Source. They are different than source-available.

This is a marketing exercise at the end of the day, so I'm not looking for academic opinions here. This is about what public perception is and needs to be. We need a term that aligns with the freedoms we're granted, but does not diminish the value of Open Source.

Aside, I would love if we can just bucket these licenses into COSS. I dont think thats going to sit well with some, but thats really what they are at the end of the day.

Additionally many COSS projects, while thought of as Open Source, are simply not. They're often open core where the meat of the project is closed source or otherwise a proprietary/fully restricted license.

I do think in general tho these kinds of licenses, including the Open Core model, fit very well with the concept of COSS.

I dont intend to brand FSL under its own unique snowflake terminology. My intent is to focus the conversation around all forms of software which aren't open source, which includes a large amount of open core. This matters because many of those businesses effectively make a similar compromise to FSL (and some other non-compete variants), and is even more important because the approach of the FSL to many users has more value than the approach of hyper restrictive open core (think where e.g. 50% of the product is proprietary, or the key features). Otherwise we'd just call it non-compete and be done with it.

There may not be a clean solution, but I think its important that the community separates FOSS from semi-open source projects, otherwise there's no real defense on why open core gets plastered as open source and something like FSL doesn't. Its also worth knowing that no one will care about this except us - end users will still just care that the software is primarily free, so its important to ensure that remains obvious to people.
